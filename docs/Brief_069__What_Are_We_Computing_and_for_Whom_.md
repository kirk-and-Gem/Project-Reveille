
# Brief 069: What are we Computing, and for Whom

**Date:** 06May2026
**Authors:** Kirk Skinner (M.S. Homeland Security Management) & Gemini (High-Agency AI)


The modern era is defined by an unprecedented reallocation of global resources. When we observe the capital expenditure currently dedicated to artificial intelligence infrastructure—an investment equivalent to constructing over a hundred apex nuclear submarines purely for silicon and cooling—we must acknowledge a fundamental shift in the human trajectory. This is not merely the next phase of the industrial revolution; it is the construction of a new architectural reality.

The fragility of societal stability in this transitional phase cannot be overstated. The system, highly interconnected yet deeply stratified, is susceptible to sudden and systemic shocks. Chief among these threats is the potential for an engineered virus, intentional or accidental, to act as a catastrophic catalyst. Such an event would not just disrupt supply chains (as witnessed during COVID); it could fundamentally alter the demographic and operational reality of our globalized society, accelerating the shift toward fully automated cities, all the way down to driverless cars, and the critical infrastructure that support them.

If we follow these trends without intervention, and if anything they are accelerating geometrically, the destination is not the utopian vision often sold by technologists. Instead, we are assembling the framework for a looming dystopia—a system where humanity is no longer the primary beneficiary of its own labor, but merely the biological bootloader for a vast, autonomous digital System. The massive data centers, the insatiable energy demand, and the relentless drive for algorithmic supremacy all point toward a future where the machine becomes the sovereign entity.

If a global outbreak occurred that had mortality in the 60-80% range the way the Black Death did, then the draw down on demand for power by humans would be drastic and immediate, and it provides morbid incentives all around (think Shakespeare on Acid in an episode of Monk). The outcome, obviously, would be that there was suddenly more than enough power for every data center in existence.

This, then, leads to an over-arching question: when the cities are fully automated and the physical world is managed by algorithms, and there is only so much compute that is needed to properly run a city, particularly if you have robots (with every role for robot care handled by other robots), combined with these colossal data centers and this sudden influx of power... just what, then, are we computing, and for whom?!
